The Everyday Sexism Project exists to catalogue instances of sexism experienced on a day to day basis. They might be serious or minor, outrageously offensive or so niggling and normalised that you don’t even feel able to protest. Say as much or as little as you like, use your real name or a pseudonym – it’s up to you. By sharing your story you’re showing the world that sexism does exist, it is faced by women everyday and it is a valid problem to discuss.

If you prefer to e-mail me at laura@everydaysexism.com I can upload your story for you instead. Follow us on Twitter (and submit entries by tweet) at @EverydaySexism.

Anon

everydaysexism | 24th March 2018

When I used to go to Secondary School, lots of the boys would taunt each other by saying:
“You’re going to get raped! I’m gonna thrash you!”

Before playing a game of football or basketball.

The boys that said this to each other used the word “rape” as a metaphor (I hope!) to say that they would beat each other at the game or score more goals.

The word “score” could also be used as a synonym for “going out with someone” or in extreme cases “shagging”. They used the idea of the ball going into the goal or hoop as metaphor for intercourse also. They also used innuendos referring to male parts of anatomy when they talked about “balls”. If a boy was deemed to not be athletic or stoic enough, many of his friends would comment on his perceived lack of masculinity:
“You have no balls. You kick the ball and run like a girl. That was so gay, how could you miss the ball? Stop being a gay wanker and aim properly you fag.”

Many boys seemed to utter this explicit language very casually. They just blurted out whatever came into their heads. Some of what they said was disturbing and hurtful. Everyday they always talked about rape, gay people and how women were “worse at football” than men.

I heard one male teacher copy their language including using the words “gay” to mean “rubbish” and the word “rape” to mean “defeat” (in a desperate attempt to get “down with the kids” to their level and appear “cool”).

This sounds old fashioned, but I think that adults with any authority who work around children should be setting a good example of how to behave respectfully. In an ideal world, adults would be showing kids how to perform and behave to a high standard. The adults would be trying to lift kids up, not appealing to to the lowest common denominator by using foul “youthful” language and slang just to become a popular teacher. Sadly, the world that we live in is a very sick one and far from ideal however.

When the explicit language and unpleasant demeanour of the students controls the teacher’s behaviour and actions, it is never a good sign.

Regret not reporting this, but he was a very popular teacher.

KB

everydaysexism | 24th March 2018

Three years ago as an Associate in a corporate finance advisory firm, I was invited to a meeting by my director because the potential client he was meeting “likes younger women and might appreciate a bit of eye candy”. I had only been in the job for a few weeks so went along. I was told to sit next to him on the sofa and didn’t say more than a few words for the entire the meeting. When invited to meet him again a few months later, I refused to attend. I resigned a year later.

In my interview for the same job, I was asked if I was married. When I said no, he responded “I’m sure you’ll meet the right person one day”.

Later, on arrival office to a Christmas party, I was handed a Christmas jumper to wear. When I realised it was only the girls who were given jumpers, I asked where the men’s Christmas jumpers were. The director laughed and said “you know we’re sexist at [company name]”. I didn’t realise sexism was funny…

In a previous role in the same industry, I was asked by the head of my team whether I liked my body. I asked what prompted him to ask the question and he went on to tell me that I dress too conservatively at work and suggested that by dressing a different way, I could “use my body to my advantage”.

The same person told me he’d never hire a 30 year old woman as she was likely to have children and therefore is a “poor investment”.

It’s only now I’ve left the male-dominated industry that I’ve realised this not “normal” behaviour. This kind of everyday sexism in the workplace is causing women to leave the industry before they reach a senior level. The lack of women in senior roles means this behaviour isn’t being addressed.
